Project #1: Relative Strength Indicator

Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a technical analysis tool first developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and published in his book New Concepts in Technical Analysis. A copy of this book is avaible for free online and is a short and interesting read; you will find many other popular analysis tools/methods and the reasoning behind their construction. Wilder is often considered the "Father of Technical Analysis" for his contributions to the field.

Although technical analysis might seem as useful as astrology, it is in fact utilized by many hedge funds as its own strategy division. The RSI in particular is used to trade not only stocks, but (as was its original purpose) futures contracts (commodities) as well.

The $RSI$, along with subsequent mathy notation in paragraph* is calculated as follows: \[RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS}\]

Here, $RS$ is the Relative Strength Factor and is the ratio of the moving averages of upward changes and downward chasnges, where each move is calculated as follows: \[ price_{now} > price_{previous} \implies \begin{cases} U = close_{now} - close_{previous}\\ D = 0 \end{cases} \] \[ price_{previous} > price_{now} \implies \begin{cases} U = 0\\ D = close_{previous} - close_{now} \end{cases} \]

The ratio is thus: \[RS = \frac{EMA\left(U, n\right)}{EMA\left(D, n\right)}\]

$EMA$ stands for Exponential Moving Average, which weighs a series of data (typically) using a factor of $\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1}$. Here, $\alpha$ is called the degree of weight decrease or smoothing factor, and there is no "correct" value to use for any of the weighted moving averages. The value used for the $EMA$ is such that it's centering is equal to that of a Simple Moving Averag (SMA).

Project #2: Price Percentage Oscillator

Price Percentage Oscillator (PPO) is a technical indicator that shows a ratio between a "short" and "long" moving averages, typically Exponential Moving Averages (EMA) relating to price. The periods of these moving averages can vary, but the typical formula is: \[PPO = \frac{EMA_{12} - EMA_{26}}{EMA_{26}} \times 100\]

Whenever the indicator is greater than 0, then the 12-period EMA, $EMA_{12}$, is greater than the 26-period EMA, $EMA_{26}$, by that percentage; whenever the indicator is less than 0, then vice versa.

I incorporate a momentum indicator along with the $PPO$ that is similar in style to the Moving Average Convergence/Divergence indicator, wherein a signal moving average is used to assist in identifying convergence and divergence moves. I typically use a very short moving average for the signal line and visually judge the momentum of the trends by a colored area difference between the $PPO$ line and the signal line.

Project #3: On-Balance-Volume Momentum Oscillator

On-Balance-Volume (OBV) is a technical indicator popularized by Joseph Granville which tracks a relationship between price and volume.

$OBV$ can be calculated with the piecewise function: \[ OBV = OBV_{prev} + \begin{cases} volume & close > close_{prev}\\ 0 & close = close_{prev}\\ -volume & close_{prev} > close \end{cases} \]

This adds up volume measures depending on whether the price closed higher or lower. The idea is to give a data value that helps to measure the importance/strength of price movements. It is based on the premise held by Granville that volume movements precede price movements; essentially, large movements in volume should cause large movements in price.

My modified version of this indicator turns it into an oscillator by taking the difference between the $OBV$ value itself and a short term moving average. This acts as a very basic filtering technique and allows for momentum analysis. Furthermore, long and short period moving averages are also added that analyze this new oscillating value.

Project #4: A Better Linear Correlation Coefficient

Correlation is a simple (but dangerous) analytical parameter that can be utilized in a number of strategies. The simplest usable correlation analysis done on financial instruments is a measuer of linear correlation, which yields a parameter called the Correlation Coefficient. This parameter can can aid in determining whether or not a collection (usually pair) of assets share any directionality.

The most commonly used parameter with financial instruments is the Pearson Correlation Coefficient ($/rho$). It is calculated as such: \[ \rho = \frac{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}}{\sqrt{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})^2}\sum{(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}} \]

While the parameter is "accurate" enough, most analytical programs tend to overlook a couple of things: - Data distribution. - Outliers and result significance.

That is, the data should be approximately normal, and should contain no outliers. Furthermore, the signifance of the data should be tested due to the nature of noise in random/stochastic varaiables. The solutions to these issues is actually simple enough!

Stock price data in particular is considered log-normally distributed; more precisely, larger moves in price are less likely to occur as the price of the asset gets smaller. While it is easy to get away with saying that large samples from stock price data will be approximately normal, the fact that stock price is assumed to be log-normal is actually a benefit; we can simply take the log of the price data!

Next, we may conduct a critical value test on the t-statistic, or t-test on the collected correlation coefficient. This can be done: \[ t = \rho \sqrt{(n - 2)/(1 - \rho^2)} \]

The main problem I ran into when building these improvements came from TradingView's platform not having any built in Cumulative Distribution Functions or table for the Student's t-distribution. However, a handy little approximation found in section 26.2.17 of Handbook of Mathematical Functions aids us in calculating the probability values for normal Cumulative Distribution Functions, or CDFs for short. This is helpful because if we restrict ourselves to "large enough" sample sizes (convention is >30), then we may use a normal CDF in place of a t-distribution as an accurate practical application. The aforementioned approximation can be seen applied in the provided chart script. Extra points go to the readers who recognized the approximation as a Taylor series approximation.

Note that while TradingView does have a "good enough" Pearson Correlation analysis script, the method in which this script is written also allows for ranking data and finding the Spearman Correlation Coefficient.

Project #5: Options Lab

Options contracts are a type of derivative security. We can fetch and analyze options-chain data using the YFinance Python package. With the help of the pandas package, this program essentially gives a breakdown of the information granted by the Black-Scholes Equation. The Black-Scholes model is what underlies the pricing of options contracts world-wide. Although it is ill-suited for pricing American options, it is one of those scenarios where "good enough" is acceptably precise.

I tend to trade options strategies often in my portfolio. Most of my knowledge is built off of the book Trading Volatility by Colin Bennett. A primary factor I look for in options is the current Implied Volatility. Having taken a college course in Mathematical Finance, I know the tedium and complexity that goes into applying the Black-Scholes Equation. This is why we get computers and "good enough" approximations to do it for us!

Newton Interpolation is a simple approximation method that assumes the model at hand follows a polynomial (which applies in our situation) and calculates the coefficients of the polynomial using the derivative of the model and divided differences. If you recall Taylor polynomials, this should look very familiar! The program that I wrote utilizes four guesses and 100 iterations of Newton Interpolation to accurately approximation the Black-Scholes formula for the given option type. As you will notice within the script, the (partial) derivative is not terribly difficult to find when trying to solve for implied volatility; the different partial derivatives of the Black-Scholes Equation are known as The Greeks. An important relationship to note when performing this is Put-Call Parity, which helps when requiring to evaluate Black-Scholes during each iteration of the approximation. The relationship states: \[ C - P = D(F - K) \]

Here, C is the call price and P is put price. The parameter D is the discount factor, which is calculated based on current interest rates and accounts for the returns missed by not allocating the price value into fixed-income strategies such as bonds. Finally, F is the forward price of the actual asset (the stock), and K is the options strike price. Note that forward price is tradtionally used as it "cancels" out with the discount factor. The forward price is essentially the return on the asset price given the return-on-investment rate of fixed-income strategies.

After solving for Implied Volatility, the program then creates a table using pandas for analysis; A few more parameters are calcualted within the script. I calcualte for Historical Volatility, which is the actualized volatility of the asset, as well as the beta value and Pearson Correlation Coefficient for the asset's return price. Here, beta is simply the return that the asset gives compared to another benchmark asset; simply put it calculates that percentage that the asset performs compared to the benchmark.

A custom but rudimentary Max Pain calculation script using YFinance is also included in my repository which pairs well with this script!

You can get the full script yourself and play around with it from my GitHub.

Project #8: Michigan RD-108 Form Automation

My parents own a small car dealership and have to frequently fill RD-108 forms. My mother is usually the one responsible for handling all of the Secretary of State paperwork involved in closing a car sale. While the state of Michigan does provide an automated Adobe form that calculates the taxes, the nature of my parents' business has them provide incentives to their customers by combinding all of the extraneous costs of the sale into the final sale price of the vehicle, which is called the "out-the-door" price. This is a big tax-calculation headache, as not everything is taxable and some things can count as deductions. The Adobe form, sadly, does not perform the necessary calculations based on being given the end price.

While the mathematics behind the calculations can be easily autoamted with a spreadsheet, my mother is not mathematically inclined and struggles with new technology (that is, anything not social media related). So far, she has managed to break three versions of the spreadsheet program that I had made for her, even with some of the new iterations including translations to Arabic (her first language).

So, I embarked on a task to figured out the best way to make a solution that she could not possibly mess up. This proved challenging at first, as the form has some requirements about the way it should be presented; that is, it's best to turn in the form filled via Adobe's framework. I was certainly no Adobe developer, but the internet had a solution for me: pdf libraries. While I have used one in the past on a C++ project, the hardest part was finding an open-source/free library that meshed well with Adobe's form formatting.

Luckily I ran into the library PDF-LIB, which worked off of TypeScript and compiled in JavaScript. Despite the lack of a server-side, I decided to use this library in Node.js and using Electron.js to build a web-like GUI. The main reasons for doing this revolved around offline access, familiarity of visuals, and ease of use. My mother can feel like she is using a browser program, without any of the distractions or hassels of a browser! Although this might seem silly and over-kill, you should see her working with tehcnology in the office.

The programs section of my GitHub provides the key Electron files and the main script which accesses the RD-108 PDF file. Note that you will need to scaffold your own Electron project and install PDF-LIB along with its dependencies/node modules.

Root Vegetables

Mushrooms are my favorite ingredient to use. Macro-nutrionally, they're a good source of fiber and protein (called mycoprotein)! I especially like the micronutrients that certain mushroom types provide. Shiitake mushrooms, for example, are actually a good source of vitamin D, just leave them to soak in the sunlight before consuming them! Mushrooms such as Cordycepts, Reishi, and Chaga provide anti-bacterial compounds as well as immune system benefits- which are so prominent that they have actually been used alongside cancer treatments as supplements! I enjoy the normal taste of mushrooms, and so prefer them minimally cooked. Simply grab some large portabella or white button mushrooms and pan steam them in shallow water/broth. Add salt and spices after the they begin to look glossy, and throw in a dash of butter at the end and flip the mushrooms around in the liquid. Add this to anything from salads to sandwhiches!

I prefer to get the majority of my protein from plants; that said, it's hard to maintain a good intake when you wish to remain active. This is why I like to make dishes centered around tofu. It's versatile and easy to flavor, even without excessive frying. My simplest method of preparing tofu: press out some of the liquid or buy extra-extra-firm tofu, and then salt and steam it in a pan with some water or broth.

My favorite nutritonal powerhouse: sprouts! I avoid most grain and legume products due to the anti-nutrient and allergenic compounds in them. Sprouts cover all macronutrients, and have a whole host of essentia vitamins and minerals that are usually not digestible or even present in non-sprouted products. The flavor of the plant is also accentuated after the sprouting proccess as well: try sprouted rye for example! There is a wide variety of sprouted products to make and try. I tend to sprout legumes at home, but not everyone has the time for the proccess. Luckily there are plenty of products out there to try! A big health improvement move for people who don't want to quit bread is to try sprouted bread. I tend to buy Ezekiel 4:9 bread, but any flourless sprouted bread is good! Adding sprouted legumes to the list such as soy bean and broccoli sprouts is an inexpensive and easy step as well! You can make a killer pizza with sprouted pizza sourdough! Just add sauce, cheese, your favorite toppings, and throw it all in an oven for 10-15 minutes.

The Standard American Diet (SAD) has an AA/EPA ratio of 11; that is, most Americans tend to consume more Omega-6 fatty acids than Omega-3. A leading cause of this is the heavy use of polyunsaturated vegetable oils in all manner of American food. It's not just the trans fats that we should worry about! This is why I tend to consume a lot of butter and ghee (clarified butter). While ghee is mainly a saturated fat, it helps me cut down my polyunsaturated fat intake while also filling my macro fat needs while also being versatile to cook with! Contrary to old studies, new studies have proven that dietary fat and cholesterol intake is unlinked to artery health; it's the inclusion of excess carbohydrates that causes issues. I tend to use Ghee as a substitute for cooking oils (cooking with polyunsaturated oils is a whole separate issue to discuss). It adds a deep rich flavor to chicken and fish dishes and sears them evenly! Add any spcies towards the end of the cooking process to add more flavor to the ghee.

One of the easiest sources of protein for humans to digest comes from eggs! They're versatile and quick to cook. I try to consume at least 5 eggs a week; good quality eggs are hard to get, though in my opinion, are worth the price! I tend to get my eggs directly from Michigan farms, but organic pasture-raised eggs (for example, Vital Farms sells these) work just as well! I prefer soft-centered eggs that are still a little gooey (over medium). Just heat up a small amount of water in a pan with any sort of spices/flavoring to make a quick broth, bring to a simmer, crack the eggs on top, and cover. The eggs are usually done when a white film forms on top of the yolk.

Versatile and store well, root vegetables are some of my favorite vegetables to cook with. I require a decent caloric intake, and root vegetables fill that need on top of providing a large array of necessary micronutrients. Care should be taken however, as some root vegetables are consumed too often and are ill-prepared! I tend to avoid potatoes in my cooking as they are a dense food, and the modern breed of the plant is high in carbohydrates and may contain some compounds that are not well tolerated; I will stil consume potatoes, but only if they are organic, boiled, and kept cold to maintain the starch structure (which is a good fiber for the gut). I will tend to also substitue potatoes with cassava starches (the stuff they make tapioca out of), as they tend to have more fiber and less agitants. The most common root vegetables I consume, however, are low-starch ones. Carrots, Beets, Parsnips, Turnips, Onions, etc. They're cheap even when organic and they store well. Try some beet stir-fry or parsnips soups!
