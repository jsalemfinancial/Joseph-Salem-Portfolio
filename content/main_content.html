Project #1: Relative Strength Indicator

Relative Strength Index is a technical analysis tool first developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and published in his book New Concepts in Technical Analysis. A copy of this book is avaible for free online and is a short and interesting read; you will find many other popular analysis tools/methods and the reasoning behind their construction. Wilder is often considered the "Father of Technical Analysis" for his contributions to the field.

Although technical analysis might seem as useful as astrology, it is in fact utilized by many hedge funds as its own strategy division. The RSI in particular is used to trade not only stocks, but (as was its original purpose) futures contracts (commodities) as well.

The RSI is calculated as follows: \[RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS}\]

Here, $RS$ is the Relative Strength Factor and is the ratio of the moving averages of upward changes and downward changes, where each move is calculated as follows: \[ price_{now} > price_{previous} \implies \begin{cases} U = close_{now} - close_{previous}\\ D = 0 \end{cases} \] \[ price_{previous} > price_{now} \implies \begin{cases} U = 0\\ D = close_{previous} - close_{now} \end{cases} \]

The ratio is thus: \[RS = \frac{EMA\left(U, n\right)}{EMA\left(D, n\right)}\]

EMA stands for Exponential Moving Average, which weighs a series of data (typically) using a factor of $\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1}$. Here, $\alpha$ is called the degree of weight decrease or smoothing factor, and there is no "correct" value to use for any of the weighted moving averages. The value used for the EMA is such that it's centering is equal to that of a Simple Moving Average (SMA).

Project #2: Price Percentage Oscillator

Price Percentage Oscillator, or PPO, is a technical indicator that shows a ratio between a "short" and "long" moving averages (typically Exponential Moving Averages) relating to price. The periods of these moving averages can vary, but the typical formula is: \[PPO = \frac{EMA_{12} - EMA_{26}}{EMA_{26}} \times 100\]

Whenever the indicator is greater than 0, then the 12-period EMA is greater than the 26-period EMA by that percentage; whenever the indicator is less than 0, then vis versa.

I personally incorporate a momentum indicator along with the PPO that is similar in style to the Moving Average Convergence/Divergence indicator, wherein a signal moving average is used to assist in identifying convergence and divergence moves. I typically use a very short moving average for the signal line and visually judge the momentum of the trends by a colored area difference between the PPO line and the signal line.

Project #3: On-Balance-Volume Momentum Oscillator

On-Balance-Volume , or OBV is a technical indicator popularized by Joseph Granville which tracks a relationship between price and volume.

OBV can be calculated with the piecewise function: \[ OBV = OBV_{prev} + \begin{cases} volume & close > close_{prev}\\ 0 & close = close_{prev}\\ -volume & close_{prev} > close \end{cases} \]

This essentially adds up volume measures depending on whether the price closed higher or lower. The idea is to give a data value that helps to measure the importance/strength of price movements. It is based on the premise held by Joseph Granville that volume movements precede price movements; essentially, large movements in volume should cause large movements in price.

Project #4: A Better Linear Correlation Coefficient

Correlation is a simple (but dangerous) analytical parameter that can be utilized in a number of strategies. The simplest usable correlation analysis done on financial instruments is a measuer of linear correlation, which yields a parameter called the Correlation Coefficient. This parameter can can aid in determining whether or not a collection (usually pair) of assets share any directionality.

The most commonly used parameter with financial instruments is the Pearson Correlation Coefficient. It is calculated as such: \[ \rho = \frac{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}}{\sqrt{\sum{(x_i - \bar{x})^2}\sum{(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}} \]

While the parameter is "accurate" enough, most analytical programs tend to overlook a couple of things:

- Data distribution.
- Outliers and result significance.

That is, the data should be approximately normal, and should contain no outliers. Furthermore, the signifance of the data should be tested due to the nature of noise in random/stochastic varaiables. The solutions to these issues is actually simple enough!

Stock price data in particular is considered log-normally distributed; more precisely, larger moves in price are less likely to occur the smaller the price of the asset is. While it is easy to get away with saying that large samples from stock price data will be approximately normal, the fact that stock price is assumed to be log-normal is actually a benefit; we can simply take the log of the price data!

Next, we may conduct a t-statistic test on the collected correlation coefficient. This can be simply done:
\[ t = \rho \sqrt{(n - 2)/(1 - \rho^2)} \]

The main problem I ran into when building these improvements came from TradingView's platform not having any built in Cumulative Distribution Functions or table for the student T-distribution. However, a handy little approximation found in section 26.2.17 of Handbook of Mathematical Functions aids us in calculating the probability values for normal CDFs. This is helpful becuase if we restrict ourselves to "large enough" sample sizes (convention is 30), then we may use a normal CDF in place of a t-distribution as an accurate practical application. The aforementioned approximation can be seen applied in the provide chart script. Extra points go to the readers who recognized the approximation as a Taylor series approximation.

Note that while TradingView does have a "good enough" Pearson Correlation analysis script, the method in which this script is written also allows for ranking data and finding the Spearman Correlation Coefficient.

Project #8: Michigan RD-108 Form Automation

My parents own a small car dealership and have to frequently fill RD-108 forms. My mother, being more socially proficient than my step-father, handles all of the Secretary of State paperwork involved in closing a car sale. While the state of Michigan does provide an automated Adobe form, the nature of my parents' business has them provide incentives to their customers by combinding all of the extraneous costs of the sale into the final sale price of the vehicle, which is called the "out-the-door" price. This is big tax headache as not everything is taxable and some things can count as deductions. The Adobe form, sadly, does not perform the necessary calculations in this direction.

While the mathematics behind the calculations can be easily autoamted with a spreadsheet, my mother is not mathematically inclined and struggles with new technology (that is, anything not social media related). She has so far manageg to break three versions of the spreadsheet program that I had made for her, even with some of the new iterations including translations to Arabic.

So I embarked on a task to figured out the best way to make a solution that she could not possibly messs up. This proved challenging at first, as the form has some requirements about the way it should be presented; that is, it's best to turn in the form filled via Adobe's framework. I was certainly no Adobe developer, but the internet had a solution for me: pdf libraries. While I have used one in the past on a C++ project, the hardest part was finding an open-source/free library that meshed well with Adobe's form formatting.

Luckily I ran into the library PDF-LIB, which worked off of TypeScript and compiled in JavaScript. Despite the lack of a server-side, I decided to use this library in Node.js and using Electron.js to build a web-like GUI. The main reasons for doing this revolved around: offline access, familrity of visuals, and ease of access. My mother can feel like she is using a browser program, without any of the distractions or hassels of a browser! Although this might seem silly and over-kill, you should see her working with tehcnology in the office.

The programs section of my GitHub provides the key Electron files and the main script which accesses the RD-108 PDF file. Note that you will need to scaffold your own Electron project and install PDF-LIB along with its dependencies/node modules.
