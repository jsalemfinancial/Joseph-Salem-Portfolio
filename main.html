Project #1: Relative Strength Indicator

Relative Strength Index is a technical analysis tool first developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and published in his book New Concepts in Technical Analysis. A copy of this book is avaible for free online and is a short and interesting read; you will find many other popular analysis tools/methods and the reasoning behind their construction. Wilder is often considered the "Father of Technical Analysis" for his contributions to the field.

Although technical analysis might seem as useful as astrology, it is in fact utilized by many hedge funds as its own strategy division. The RSI in particular is used to trade not only stocks, but (as was its original purpose) futures contracts (commodities) as well.

The RSI is calculated as follows: \[RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS}\]

Here, $RS$ is the Relative Strength Factor and is the ratio of the moving averages of upward changes and downward changes, where each move is calculated as follows: \[ price_{now} > price_{previous} \implies \begin{cases} U = close_{now} - close_{previous}\\ D = 0 \end{cases} \] \[ price_{previous} > price_{now} \implies \begin{cases} U = 0\\ D = close_{previous} - close_{now} \end{cases} \]

The ratio is thus: \[RS = \frac{EMA\left(U, n\right)}{EMA\left(D, n\right)}\]

EMA stands for Exponential Moving Average, which weighs a series of data (typically) using a factor of $\alpha = \frac{2}{N+1}$. Here, $\alpha$ is called the degree of weight decrease or smoothing factor, and there is no "correct" value to use for any of the weighted moving averages. The value used for the EMA is such that it's centering is equal to that of a Simple Moving Average (SMA).

Project #2: Price Percentage Oscillator

Price Percentage Oscillator, or PPO, is a technical indicator that shows a ratio between a "short" and "long" moving averages (typically Exponential Moving Averages) relating to price. The periods of these moving averages can vary, but the typical formula is: \[PPO = \frac{EMA_{12} - EMA_{26}}{EMA_{26}} \times 100\]

Whenever the indicator is greater than 0, then the 12-period EMA is greater than the 26-period EMA by that percentage; whenever the indicator is less than 0, then vis versa.

I personally incorporate a momentum indicator along with the PPO that is similar in style to the Moving Average Convergence/Divergence indicator, wherein a signal moving average is used to assist in identifying convergence and divergence moves. I typically use a very short moving average for the signal line and visually judge the momentum of the trends by a colored area difference between the PPO line and the signal line.
